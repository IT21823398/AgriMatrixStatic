Research Topic - Detecting and Monitoring Agricultural Lands in Sri Lanka using Remote Sensing and Deep Learning

Sri Lanka’s agriculture is a cornerstone of its economy, sustaining millions of livelihoods across the island. Yet, challenges like unpredictable weather, land-use changes, and inefficient monitoring make it difficult to manage farmlands effectively. Traditional methods of tracking crop growth and land conditions are slow and often inaccurate — especially across large or remote areas.

Our project uses remote sensing and deep learning to transform how agricultural lands are detected and monitored in Sri Lanka. By analyzing satellite imagery from sources such as Sentinel-2 and Landsat, we can automatically identify cultivated areas, track vegetation health, and detect land-use changes with remarkable precision.

Through deep learning models like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), the system learns to recognize different crop patterns, soil textures, and vegetation indices — turning complex image data into meaningful insights. This technology enables continuous, island-wide monitoring of farmlands, providing valuable information for farmers, researchers, and policymakers to make informed decisions that boost productivity and promote sustainable agriculture.

Literature Review

Detecting and Monitoring Varieties of Crops in Cultivated Lands

Remote sensing technology combined with machine learning algorithms enables scientists to detect and track different crop types throughout extensive agricultural areas. The research conducted by Lu et al. (2023) demonstrated that time-series satellite imagery combined with deep learning architectures can accurately distinguish crop types and growth stages, even under varying environmental conditions. The research conducted by Chandrasiri et al. (The authors (2023) used Sentinel-2 and Landsat data to generate rice field maps throughout Sri Lanka which demonstrated that multispectral imagery works well for large-scale crop monitoring operations. The methods enable precision agriculture because they allow farmers to track crop health and distribution patterns at particular times.


Detection and Classification of Agricultural Lands

Deep learning-based classification systems serve as primary tools for agricultural land use identification which includes paddy and tea and coconut plantations. Turkoglu (2021) demonstrated that Sentinel-2 data can be processed through convolutional neural networks to detect agricultural land parcels and estimate their production output. Real-time agricultural area classification becomes possible through automated systems which use these networks to perform spatial productivity analysis between different regions. The methods allow for real-time crop-type detection and land monitoring which helps policymakers and managers make better decisions.


Aerial Land Measurement and Vegetation Health Analysis

Combining aerial remote sensing, geospatial modeling, and machine learning has proven highly effective for evaluating vegetation health and measuring cultivated areas. Gholizadeh et al. (The authors of (2025) studied precision agriculture systems to find that NDVI and EVI vegetation indices successfully identify crop stress and canopy health issues before they become severe. Complementary research by Herath et al. (The research by (2024) showed how UAV-based imagery works for paddy field monitoring and weed detection in Sri Lanka through the combination of aerial data with ground-based verification. The implemented methods enhance both land measurement accuracy throughout various regions and vegetation data analysis capabilities.


Crop Yield Prediction and Alert System

The fusion of remote sensing indices with climatic parameters and advanced machine learning models has greatly improved yield prediction accuracy. Dissanayake et al. (The authors in (2023) showed that Support Vector Machines and Artificial Neural Networks and LSTMs produce superior results than statistical approaches for crop yield prediction. Ekanayake et al. (The authors in (2022) used Gaussian Process Regression to forecast Sri Lankan paddy yields based on rainfall and temperature data which produced excellent results. The models generate precise yield predictions which enable warning systems to detect both abandoned and vulnerable agricultural areas.


Reference Summary

Reserach Gap

Efficiently managing Sri Lanka’s agricultural lands is challenged by manual, time-consuming methods. There is a need for an automated system to detect and classify crop varieties, provide aerial land measurements and vegetation health analysis, and issue alerts for uncultivated lands or crop changes. Integrating satellite imagery and deep learning can enable real-time monitoring, support precision agriculture, and optimize crop management.

Reserach Problems


Challenges in Multi-Class Crop Classification

How can crop varieties such as Tea, Rubber, Paddy, Coconut, and Palm in Sri Lanka’s cultivated lands be accurately detected and monitored using satellite imagery and deep learning techniques, while overcoming challenges of environmental variability, crop similarities, and data limitations?


Automated Crop Productivity Classification Impact

How can Sri Lanka overcome the absence of an automated, satellite-based system that provides real-time, spatially explicit crop productivity data for paddy, tea, and coconut, instead of relying on manual, error-prone data collection methods that hinder timely decision-making and effective agricultural management?


Challenges in Monitoring Crop Health

Farmers in Sri Lanka face significant challenges in accurately measuring cropland and monitoring crop health. Traditional land measurement methods are slow, labor-intensive, and often struggle to account for slopes, resulting in inaccurate area estimations. At the same time, vegetation monitoring is mostly reactive, relying on current NDVI snapshots rather than predictive forecasting, and there is a lack of farmer-friendly visualization tools.


Lacking an Automated Crop Yield Prediction System

Sri Lanka lacks an automated, reliable crop yield prediction and uncultivated land monitoring system. Current practices rely on manual surveys, which are slow, error-prone, and provide limited spatial/temporal coverage


Reserach Objectives


Detecting and Monitoring Varieties of Crops in Cultivated Lands

Detecting and monitoring varieties of crop in cultivated lands using satellite imagery and classification techniques involves a combination of remote sensing, data analysis, and ML.


Detection and Classification of Agricultural Lands

Develop an automated satellite-based system to monitor and classify paddy, tea, and coconut plantations in Sri Lanka into high, medium, and low productivity levels using deep learning and real-time web visualization.


Aerial Land Measurement and Vegetation Health Analysis

Design and implement an integrated system for predictive vegetation health analysis, province-wise cropland mapping and area calculation, and province-wise land cover classification and area calculation, by combining remote sensing data, geospatial analysis, and advanced machine learning models.


Crop Yield Prediction and Alert System

To develop crop yield prediction and alert system for Sri Lanka that predicts seasonal crop yields at district level (Mainly for paddy), integrates remote sensing indices (NDVI, soil moisture) with climate factors (rainfall, temperature), provides an early warning alert mechanism for uncultivated lands and crop changes, and delivers outputs through a user-friendly web-based dashboard.

Methodology

The AgriMatrix system integrates remote sensing, geospatial analytics, and deep learning to enable large-scale detection, monitoring, and productivity assessment of agricultural lands across Sri Lanka. The workflow combines data collection, preprocessing, model development, system integration, and visualization.


1. Data Acquisition and Preprocessing

The collection includes satellite data from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8 and UAV information for various agricultural districts. The Python libraries NumPy and Pandas provide efficient array and tabular operations which support cloud masking and atmospheric correction tasks in the cloud. The analysis uses NDVI and EVI and NDWI vegetation indices to obtain spectral data which shows vegetation health and soil moisture levels. The SMOTE technique serves to balance datasets with uneven crop distributions because it enables models to achieve improved generalization performance.


2. Feature Engineering and Model Development

The backend system uses FastAPI and Flask to create a modular structure which enables scalable deployment and API-based data exchange. The system uses FastAPI to handle model inference requests and image uploads and data queries but Flask serves as the dashboard visualization platform which delivers processed analytics to the frontend. The system stores data in MongoDB because this database management system efficiently manages geospatial JSON structures and time-series crop metrics.


3. System Architecture and Backend Integration

The implemented methods enhance both land measurement accuracy throughout various regions and vegetation data analysis capabilities.


4. Visualization and User Interface

The web-based visualization layer uses React and CSS to create an interactive platform which enables users to view classified land parcels and health maps and yield predictions. The system updates vegetation indices and alerts and analytics in real time through its dynamic data rendering functionality. The dashboard shows project milestones through its vertical timeline design which allows users to monitor progress through analytics. The system enables decision-making for agricultural authorities through its ability to integrate mapping APIs which allows users to overlay classified layers with district boundaries and yield forecasts.


5. System Output and Evaluation

The system delivers three core outputs:

  • Land classification maps showing cultivated crops and productivity zones.
  • Vegetation health analytics with province-wise summaries.
  • Yield prediction and alert notifications highlighting low-yield or uncultivated areas.
